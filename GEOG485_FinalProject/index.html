US Disaster Declarations Analysis

About the Project

Final Project - GEOG 485

Abstract

Numerous natural disasters experienced in the United States every year, as tracked by the Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA), have significant impacts on everyday Americans’ lives. Every year, disasters ranging from wildfires and floods to biological events such as the COVID-19 pandemic shake the country, and in the face of climate change, these are only predicted to increase in severity, intensity, and frequency. FEMA has tracked all natural disasters across the US annually by county since 1953, enabling us in this project to visually represent the occurrences of these disasters over time to showcase areas with the highest natural disaster risk, how different regions may have changed over time due to various factors such as climate, land use change, and population, and to highlight the relationship between different geographies and different disaster types. The coalescence of this data into one interactive figure enables people to more easily understand how they may be impacted by natural disasters, how natural disaster statistics have changed over time, and to predict how they may continue to change. With the threat of climate change and its intense effects on natural disasters continuing to worsen, having this accessible visualization of data available to the public is crucial.

How to Use

When a county is selected from the map, a pie chart displaying the number of disaster declarations and their types will appear below. The first pie chart corresponds to the state the county resides in, while the one below that is for the county itself.

Use the slider above to adjust the time range that displays on the map below, which shows the disaster rate (average disasters per year) for each US county

Use the dropdown selector below to switch between different data classification types (Quantile, Equal Interval, or Natural Breaks)
